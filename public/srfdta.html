SURF ZONE FORECAST
NATIONAL WEATHER SERVICE HONOLULU HI
300 PM HST MON JAN 21 2013

OAHU-
300 PM HST MON JAN 21 2013

Surf along north facing shores will be 10 to 15 feet this afternoon building to 20 to 30 feet for this evening through early Tuesday.

Surf along west facing shores will be 8 to 12 feet this afternoon building to 15 to 20.

Surf along east facing shores will be 1 to 3 feet this afternoon, building to 3 to 5 feet tonight.

Surf along south facing shores will be 0 to 2 feet through Tuesday.

Outlook through Sunday Jan 27: a large northwest swell will peak tonight, bringing warning level surf to north and west facing shores tonight into Tuesday morning. This swell will then lower through Wednesday. Smaller, moderate northwest swells are expected from Thursday into Friday, and late Saturday into Sunday.

Surf heights are forecast heights of the face or front of waves. The surf forecast is based on the significant wave height, the average height of the one third largest waves, in the zone of maximum refraction. Some waves may be more than twice as high as the significant wave height. Expect to encounter rip currents in or near any surf zone.

DISCUSSION:

Summary: coinciding episodes for north shores making for confused breakers.

Detailed: mid Friday on northern shores has extra-large breakers from 290-310 degrees with 17-24 second wave periods. The highest breakers under such conditions occur on outer reefs. Near shore heights are lower. There are also declining smaller, shorter-period waves from 310-325 degrees left over from the Thursday 1/17 episode and steady windswell of 7-11 seconds from 330-010 degrees. The mix of episodes is expected to continue over the weekend.

The dominant swell on 1/18 is from a long-lived, massive low pressure in the NW Pacific 1/14-18. The central surface pressure dipped to 932 mb late Monday HST 1/14. The system formed near Tokyo 1/14 and accelerated to 40°N, 160°E by late Monday, where the system stalled and broadened.

The strongest winds were mid Monday to mid Tuesday with storm- to hurricane-force winds over the 295-320 degree band. The extreme winds over the large fetch elevated the wave periods. Buoy 51101 WNW of Kauai Thursday morning HST 1/17 registered a tickle of energy in the 30 second band, significant energy centered on 26 seconds, and a substantial around 24 seconds. The direction has been partially shadowed by Kauai on Oahu Thursday into Friday. Buoy 51101 on Friday morning HST 1/18 shows a decrease in the extra-long wave period energy greater than 20 seconds, as the dominant energy focuses within 17-19 seconds, and the direction slowly adds more NW component. This suggests less Kauai shadow for the remainder of the episode.

The occluded low pressure in the NW Pacific steadily weakened starting mid Tuesday 1/15, though still maintained a huge fetch area of severe gales to storm-force winds over the 305-320 degree band, with the head of the fetch about 2000 nm away. As the low pressured weakened into Thursday, the center drifted NW, shrinking the fetch to mostly gales just east of the northern Kuril Islands and SE Kamchatka. On 1/18, swell trains from this long-lived system stretch from Hawaii to the Kurils, or roughly 2800 nm long. For the final significant shorter-period swell near the Kurils on 1/18, it would take a week to arrive locally, making quite a long conveyor belt from a single source.

The extra-large surf is expected to continue on Saturday 1/19 with the dominant direction slowly veering from 310 to 315 degrees. From the remote source, high surf would continue on Sunday, to marginally high Monday, then slowly lower Tuesday through Thursday. However, several other sources are expected to overlap the remote source.

Weather charts on 1/18 show a pair of new sources aimed at Hawaii. A front is approaching Hawaii from the north as it weakens. Strong to near gale winds to the north of the front have nosed to within 400 nm on Friday morning HST 1/18. The fetch of similar winds are modelled to shift east over the weekend. This should add moderate windswell from 330-360 degrees on Saturday and from 350-020 degrees on Sunday.

A gale low is forming near 170°W, 40°N on Friday morning HST 1/18. Models show a fetch of gales aiming highest seas just NE of Hawaii, with the fetch about 500 nm away early Saturday, then east of the Hawaii swell window by late Saturday as the low moves east. The proximity should add high surf locally, building Saturday night from 330-345 degrees, peaking Sunday morning from 330-350 degrees, then dropping steadily Sunday night into Monday from 340-360 degrees.

The combined effects of the remote and nearby source could keep marginally extra-large breakers through Sunday. The nearby source would contain a significant amount of shorter-period swell, making for a disorganized breaker pattern.

A new low pressure has formed near Tokyo on Friday 1/18. Models show the system gaining girth and strength as it reaches the dateline on 1/19 with storm-force winds. It is modelled to race to the gulf of Alaska, so it should be a short-lived episode.

Long period forerunners are due Monday afternoon, with high surf just after sundown 1/21 from 305-320 degrees. The episode should peak to extra-large heights near dawn Tuesday from 310-330 degrees. The episode is predicted to fall to moderate levels on Wednesday 1/23 from within 310-350 degrees.

Mid Friday on eastern shores has breakers for northerly exposures. Similar surf is expected through the weekend from the sources described above. The highest for such zones are expected Sunday.

See the latest NWS state weather forecast discussion for an explanation of the varying local wind field 1/19-23.

A fetch of fresh trades to the ENE to NE of Hawaii starting 1/21 should build a windswell locally starting Monday. Moderate surf should slowly grow to near high levels by Wednesday from within 40-70 degrees.

Mid Friday on southern shores has small breakers from refracting swell. No surf from southern hemispheric sources is expected through the period.

Into the long range, storm-force winds in the southern Tasman 1/17-18 could make for small, long-period breakers locally 1/26-27 from 208-220 degrees.

In the northern hemisphere, models keep an active pace of fast-moving, winter caliber low pressures spaced about 3 days apart 1/21-25, that should give short-lived NW episodes locally peaking within moderate to low-end high roughly 1/25 and 1/28. Gentle to moderate trades are estimated for 1/24-25. A surface low pressure 1/21-23 near 140°W, 25°N, should enhance the trades to its north, keeping moderate or higher shorter-period swell locally 1/24-26 from 60-80 degrees.

Long range forecasts are subject to low confidence.

This collaborative forecast will resume on Tuesday, January 22.
